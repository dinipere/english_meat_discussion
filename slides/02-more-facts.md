## More facts

# What matters most?

- **25-33%**  
  Global emissions from food

- **beef & lamb**  
  usually the highest meat impact

- **pork**  
  lower than beef, higher than most plants

- **chicken & fish**  
  often lower, but still not zero

- **transport: 5%**  
  small part of food emissions

- **plant proteins**  
  peas, beans and tofu are much lower

<div class="footnote">Source: Our World in Data</div>

Speaker notes:

This slide shows the most important idea for the climate part of the discussion.

It is not only about how far food travels.

About 25 to 33 percent of global emissions come from food.

But transport is only a small part of food emissions.

Only about 5 percent comes from transport.

So the question is not only: Is the food local?

The bigger question is: What type of food is it?

Different types of meat are not the same.

Beef and lamb usually have the highest climate impact.

They produce methane, and they often need a lot of land and feed.

Pork is normally lower than beef and lamb.

But it is still higher than many plant-based foods.

Chicken and fish are often lower than pork and beef.

But they still have an impact, for example from feed, farming, energy and transport.

Plant proteins like peas, beans or tofu are usually much lower.

This is why the discussion is not simply meat or no meat.

It also matters which kind of meat people eat, how often they eat it, and what they replace it with.

For example, eating beef every day has a very different impact from eating chicken once a week or choosing a plant-based meal.

This gives us a more realistic discussion.

People do not all have to make the same choice, but small changes can still make a difference.
